Why this archive exists

Engineering failures are not accidents.
They are decisions.

Behind every collapsed bridge, ruptured reactor, and sunken vessel is a chain of choices — design compromises, ignored warnings, costs approved at the expense of safety margins. The structures fail, but the failure begins long before the steel buckles or the seal cracks.

This archive makes those chains visible — not as a record of tragedy, but as a working reference for engineers, students, and anyone who wants to understand what actually goes wrong, and why the same patterns keep recurring across decades and disciplines.

The Pattern

"The engineers voted no.
Management launched anyway."

The night before the Challenger launch, engineers at Morton Thiokol formally recommended against the flight. Temperatures at Cape Canaveral were forecast to drop below 18°F — far outside the tested range for the O-ring seals. They had the data. They made the recommendation. Their managers overrode them, and NASA launched at 11:38 AM on January 28, 1986.

This pattern — a technical warning dismissed by organisational pressure — is not unique to Challenger. It appears, in some form, in the majority of disasters documented in this archive. The Bhopal plant had multiple safety systems offline simultaneously. The Fern Hollow Bridge had been rated "poor" for years. The Champlain Towers had outstanding repair orders since 1992. The Columbia foam strike was classified as "not a safety issue" by management thirteen days before reentry.

The engineering is rarely the problem. The org chart is.

The Interval

Seven years.
The average time between
a known defect and a collapse.

The I-35W bridge in Minneapolis was rated "structurally deficient" in 1990. It collapsed in 2007. The gusset plates that failed were undersized from the original 1967 design — a calculation error that was never caught in 40 years of inspections because inspections looked at surface condition, not load capacity.

This is the most consistent finding across this archive: the gap between documented concern and catastrophic outcome is not measured in days. It is measured in budget cycles, in political terms, in the quiet accumulation of deferred maintenance. Inspectors file the reports. The reports enter a queue. The queue has a funding problem.

The Tacoma Narrows bridge oscillated visibly for four months before it fell. Engineers, drivers, and local press all documented it. A film crew was on-site the morning it collapsed. Nobody was surprised — they just hadn't fixed it yet.

The Repetition

The same failure has a
different name every decade.

Resonance destroyed the Tacoma Narrows Bridge in 1940. Sixty years later, the Millennium Bridge in London had to be closed two days after opening because pedestrian footfall caused near-identical lateral oscillation. Different country, different century, different engineering team — the same physics, the same oversight.

The Hartford Civic Center roof collapsed in 1978 under its own weight, not under snow load. The space frame design had never been properly analysed for the full combination of stresses. In 2003, a terminal roof at Charles de Gaulle Airport failed under similar circumstances. The investigators noted the Hartford collapse in their report.

This archive exists because the engineering community has a short memory. Post-disaster reports are thorough. The recommendations are reasonable. The implementation is optional. What gets built into the next generation of codes is a fraction of what gets learned — and even that knowledge erodes within two professional generations. The disasters in this database are not historical curiosities. Several of them are still being repeated.

Personal Note

Why I Made This

When my request to start an engineering club was denied (the administration pointed to robotics and science olympiad as "close enough") I realized no one was going to make space for the discipline I cared about, so I built that space myself. I created a website cataloging 50 engineering disasters spanning 176 years, from the 1847 Dee Bridge collapse in Chester, England to the 2023 Titan Submersible implosion.

What drew me in wasn't the spectacle of failure. It was the pattern underneath it. The more I researched, the more I noticed that these weren't freak accidents scattered randomly across history; they were repeated lessons that engineers kept having to relearn, sometimes at the cost of entire communities.

Building the site turned a rejection into something I couldn't put down. I found that 80% of the disasters I cataloged were deemed preventable in hindsight, failures where someone saw the warning and was ignored or overruled. That single statistic reshaped how I think about engineering entirely: the discipline isn't just about making things work, it's about building cultures where the person who spots the crack isn't afraid to say so.

Daniel Chang
