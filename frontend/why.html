Why I Made This

When my request to start an engineering club was denied (the administration pointed to robotics and science olympiad as "close enough") I realized no one was going to make space for the discipline I cared about, so I built that space myself. I created a website cataloging 50 engineering disasters spanning 176 years, from the 1847 Dee Bridge collapse in Chester, England to the 2023 Titan Submersible implosion.

What drew me in wasn't the spectacle of failure. It was the pattern underneath it. The more I researched, the more I noticed that these weren't freak accidents scattered randomly across history; they were repeated lessons that engineers kept having to relearn, sometimes at the cost of entire communities.

Building the site turned a rejection into something I couldn't put down. I found that 80% of the disasters I cataloged were deemed preventable in hindsight, failures where someone saw the warning and was ignored or overruled. That single statistic reshaped how I think about engineering entirely: the discipline isn't just about making things work, it's about building cultures where the person who spots the crack isn't afraid to say so.

Daniel Chang
